Project Overview

Autoresearch De Novo: Protein Binder Design with Test-Time Compute

April 2026 — Ongoing

The Research Question

How should we allocate test-time compute for de novo protein binder design? Specifically, how do beam search configuration choices interact with protein target properties — and can we show that no single search strategy is universally optimal?

This project uses Proteina-Complexa (ICLR 2026 Oral), a flow-matching generative model for atomistic protein binder design. We systematically sweep beam search configurations across diverse protein targets to understand the interplay between search strategy and target difficulty.

The Pipeline

Proteina-Complexa uses partially latent flow matching to generate binder structures, followed by AlphaFold2 reward scoring:

[Target PDB + Hotspots] → Flow Matching (400 steps) → Beam Search → AF2 Scoring → Candidate PDBs
Generate

Flow-matching diffusion samples binder structures in partially latent space (backbone CA + local latents).

Search

Beam search with branching at checkpoints. 5 configurations tested: balanced, length-explorer, deep-exploiter, early-brancher, fine-selector.

Score

AlphaFold2 multimer scores each candidate. Reward = −i_PAE (interface predicted aligned error).

Refine

Sequence hallucination for test-time optimization (not yet applied in sweep).

Hardware: 8× NVIDIA H100 80GB  |  Models: complexa.ckpt (2.8 GB) + complexa_ae.ckpt (3.9 GB)  |  Targets: 44 pre-configured proteins

Beam Search Sweep: 5 Configs × 10 Proteins

We tested 5 beam search configurations across 10 diverse protein targets (50 experiments total), producing 26,560 samples (1,600 finals).

Key Finding

E_fine_selector (frequent fine-grained pruning) is the best overall strategy. But the optimal config varies per protein — see the config diversity analysis below.

The Central Result: No Universal Best Config

While E_fine_selector wins 7/10 proteins overall, the optimal search strategy depends on both the protein target and the selection criterion:

  • 4/10 proteins have different winners when switching from top-1 (best single sample) to top-16 (best candidate pool)
  • Hard targets (BetV1, CD45) are highly config-sensitive (CV > 0.5); easy targets (IFNAR2) are config-insensitive (CV = 0.03)
  • Different configs produce different sequences — inter-config identity is lower than intra-config identity

Implication

For production binder campaigns on hard targets: run 2–3 configs and pool candidates. This yields more diverse, higher-quality candidate sets than running one config with equivalent compute. Search strategy matters most precisely when the target is hardest.

What's Next

  1. Add refinement — sequence hallucination on E_fine_selector results (expected to dramatically improve pLDDT and i_PAE)
  2. Full pipeline — filter → evaluate → analyze for designability metrics (scRMSD, ProteinMPNN recovery)
  3. Hybrid config — combine E's frequent pruning with D's wide branching
  4. Multi-config pooling study — formally compare single-config vs multi-config candidate pools

Day 3

No Universal Best Config: Per-Protein Optimality & Sequence Diversity

April 5, 2026

The Claim

The optimal beam search configuration is protein-dependent. The winner changes depending on both the target and the selection criterion (top-1 vs top-16 pool). Different configs explore different regions of sequence space.

Why this matters

If you plan to experimentally test 16 candidates, optimizing for top-1 gives you the wrong config for 40% of targets. The selection criterion must match the experimental plan.

Sequence Diversity: Configs Explore Different Space

Beyond finding different quality binders, configs generate structurally and sequentially distinct candidates:

  • Length distributions differ: B_length_explorer samples 16 binder lengths (broadest); C_deep_exploiter samples only 2 (narrowest). Same target, very different structural coverage.
  • Inter-config sequence identity is lower than intra-config: Sequences from different configs share less identity than sequences within the same config. The search strategies explore different regions of the design landscape.
  • Amino acid composition varies subtly: Different configs show 2–5% differences in hydrophobic/polar/charged residue fractions, reflecting different design biases.
